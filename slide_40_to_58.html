1. Introduction to Waveform Encoding

Waveform coding schemes aim to reproduce the source's continuous waveform at the destination with minimal distortion. The properties of the waveform (not the semantic structure of the source) guide the design.

Four main methods are covered:

  • PCM: Pulse Code Modulation
  • DPCM: Differential PCM
  • DM: Delta Modulation
  • ADM: Adaptive DM

Pulse Code Modulation (PCM)

A pulse code modulator consists of three stages: Sampler → Quantizer → Encoder.

Assuming a signal bounded by \([-x_{\max}, x_{\max}]\), sampled above the Nyquist rate (\(f_s \ge 2W\)), we map the infinite continuous values into \(N = 2^v\) discrete levels.

\[ \text{Step Size } (\Delta) = \frac{2x_{\max}}{N} = \frac{2x_{\max}}{2^v} \]

2. Quantization Noise & SQNR

In Uniform PCM, the quantized values are the midpoints of the regions. Thus, the quantization error \(\tilde{x}\) is uniformly distributed between \([-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]\).

🔍 Deep Dive: The 6dB Rule

One of the most crucial takeaways is the relationship between the number of bits (\(v\)) and the Signal-to-Quantization Noise Ratio (SQNR).

\[ SQNR \approx 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{P_x}{x_{\max}^2} \right) + 6v + 4.8 \text{ dB} \]

Why 6dB per bit? Every time you add 1 bit to your encoder, you double the number of quantization levels (\(N\)). This cuts the step size \(\Delta\) in half. Since noise power is proportional to \(\Delta^2\), halving \(\Delta\) quarters the noise power. In decibels, a factor of 4 is exactly \(10 \log_{10}(4) \approx 6 \text{ dB}\). Each extra bit improves your signal quality by 6dB!

Bandwidth of PCM

If we sample at \(f_s\) and use \(v\) bits per sample, the bit rate is \(R = v f_s\). The minimum baseband transmission bandwidth required is:

\[ BW_{\text{req}} = \frac{R}{2} = \frac{v f_s}{2} \]

3. Nonuniform PCM & Companding

Uniform PCM works well when signal values are uniformly distributed. However, speech signals linger near zero amplitude most of the time. Nonuniform PCM gives more finely spaced quantization levels to small amplitudes and coarsely spaced levels to large amplitudes.

We achieve this using Companding (Compressing + Expanding). We pass the signal through a logarithmic compressor, quantize it uniformly, and then expand it at the receiver.

Interactive: \(\mu\)-Law Compander Curve

Adjust the \(\mu\) parameter to see how the compression curve bows, allocating more dynamic range to lower amplitudes.

Higher \(\mu\) causes more aggressive compression. \(\mu=0\) yields a linear response (Uniform PCM). \(\mu=255\) is the standard for North American telecommunications.

4. DPCM & Delta Modulation

DPCM quantizes the difference between adjacent samples rather than the samples themselves. Because samples are highly correlated, the difference is small, requiring fewer bits.

Delta Modulation (DM) is the extreme case of DPCM using only a 1-bit quantizer. It simply asks: "Is the signal going up or down?" and steps by \(\pm\Delta\).

⚠️ The Most Confusing Part: DM Trade-offs

Delta Modulation suffers from two distinct types of distortion based on the chosen step size \(\Delta\):

  • Slope Overload: If \(\Delta\) is too small, the staircase cannot climb fast enough to keep up with rapid signal changes.
  • Granular Noise: If \(\Delta\) is too large, the staircase violently overshoots and oscillates around slow or flat regions of the signal.

5. Adaptive Delta Modulation (ADM)

How do we fix the Slope Overload vs. Granular Noise dilemma? By making \(\Delta\) adaptive!

If the encoder outputs the same bit consecutively (e.g., UP, UP, UP), it means the signal is rising rapidly. ADM will dynamically increase \(\Delta\) to catch up. If the bits alternate (e.g., UP, DOWN, UP, DOWN), it means we are in a flat region, so ADM will decrease \(\Delta\) to reduce granular noise.

\[ \Delta_n = \Delta_{n-1} K^{\epsilon_n \times \epsilon_{n-1}} \]

Where \(K > 1\), and \(\epsilon\) is the sign of the quantizer output.
