Chapter Summary
Chapter 4

Analog to Digital Conversion

Reference: Proakis & Salehi (2014), Fundamentals of Communication Systems, Chapter 7

In earlier chapters you learned how to transmit analog signals using analog modulation (AM, FM, PM). But digital communication systems offer huge advantages:

  • Higher noise immunity — digital signals can be regenerated perfectly at repeaters
  • Flexible bandwidth–power tradeoff — you can trade bits for SNR
  • Encryption & anti-jamming — easy to apply cryptographic techniques
  • VLSI implementation — digital circuits are cheap to mass-produce

The first step in any digital communication system is converting the analog source signal into a digital (binary) representation. That's what this chapter is about.

Sampling — Take "snapshots" of the analog signal at regular time intervals \(T_s\). The sampled values can still be any real number (infinite precision).
Quantization — Round each sample to the nearest value from a finite set of allowed levels. This introduces a small error called quantization noise.
Encoding — Assign a unique binary codeword (e.g. 011) to each quantization level. If there are \(N\) levels, you need \(v = \log_2 N\) bits per sample.
Common confusion

Why can't we skip quantization? After sampling, each value is still a real number with infinite decimal digits. You can't transmit infinite precision digitally — you'd need infinite bits! Quantization is the step that makes the representation finite, at the cost of a small, controlled error.

Part I — Sampling

Intuition & Motivation

Imagine two signals:

  • \(x_1(t)\) — a smooth, slowly changing signal (low-frequency content). You only need a few samples to "sketch" it.
  • \(x_2(t)\) — a rapidly wiggling signal (high-frequency content). You need many more samples to capture the wiggles.

The key insight: the faster the signal changes (higher bandwidth), the more frequently you must sample it.

Think of it like this

Imagine filming a spinning wheel. If your camera's frame rate is too slow, the wheel appears to spin backwards — that's the visual equivalent of aliasing! You need enough frames (samples) per rotation (cycle) to correctly capture the motion.

The Sampling Theorem

Theorem (Nyquist–Shannon)

If a signal \(x(t)\) is bandlimited to \(W\) Hz — meaning \(X(f) = 0\) for all \(|f| \geq W\) — then it is completely determined by its samples taken at intervals:

\[ T_s \leq \frac{1}{2W} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad f_s \geq 2W \]

The minimum sampling rate \(f_s = 2W\) is called the Nyquist rate. The corresponding sampling interval is the Nyquist interval \(T_s = \frac{1}{2W}\).

Students often ask

"What does bandlimited mean exactly?" — A bandlimited signal has zero energy at frequencies above \(W\). In practice, no real signal is perfectly bandlimited, so we use an anti-aliasing filter (a lowpass filter with cutoff \(W\)) before sampling to enforce this condition.

What happens at exactly the Nyquist rate?

At exactly \(f_s = 2W\), reconstruction is theoretically possible but requires an ideal lowpass filter (a "brick wall" in the frequency domain and an infinitely long \(\text{sinc}\) in time) — which is physically impossible to build. That's why practical systems always sample above the Nyquist rate.

Reconstruction Formula

Given samples \(\{x(nT_s)\}\), the original signal can be reconstructed exactly (assuming \(f_s \geq 2W\)):

\[ x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x(nT_s) \;\text{sinc}\!\left(\frac{t}{T_s} - n\right) \]

(Special case when sampling at exactly the Nyquist rate: \(W' = W = \frac{1}{2T_s}\))

What is sinc?

The sinc function is defined as \(\text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x}\). Its key property: it equals 1 at \(x = 0\) and 0 at every other integer. This means each sinc "picks out" exactly one sample value and contributes zero at all other sample times — perfect interpolation!

More General Reconstruction

The general formula (for oversampling, where \(f_s > 2W\)) uses an arbitrary \(W'\) where \(W \leq W' \leq \frac{1}{T_s} - W\):

\[ x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} 2W'T_s \cdot x(nT_s)\;\text{sinc}\!\bigl(2W'(t - nT_s)\bigr) \]
Why do students get confused here?

Linear interpolation vs. sinc interpolation: Connecting samples with straight lines (linear interpolation) gives only an approximation. Using sinc functions gives perfect reconstruction — but only if the sampling theorem conditions are met. The sinc-based formula is a mathematical ideal; in practice, we approximate it with realizable filters.

Proof of the Sampling Theorem

Click to expand the full frequency-domain proof

Step 1 — Impulse Sampling

Model sampling as multiplication with an impulse train:

\[ x_\delta(t) = x(t) \cdot \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t - nT_s) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x(nT_s)\,\delta(t - nT_s) \]

Step 2 — Fourier Transform

The Fourier transform of a periodic impulse train is another impulse train (scaled by \(1/T_s\)):

\[ \mathcal{F}\left\{\sum_{n} \delta(t - nT_s)\right\} = \frac{1}{T_s} \sum_{n} \delta\!\left(f - \frac{n}{T_s}\right) \]

Multiplying in time ↔ convolution in frequency:

\[ X_\delta(f) = X(f) * \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_n \delta\!\left(f - \frac{n}{T_s}\right) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} X\!\left(f - \frac{n}{T_s}\right) \]

Step 3 — Interpretation

The spectrum of the sampled signal is just copies (replicas) of \(X(f)\) shifted by multiples of \(f_s = 1/T_s\), each scaled by \(1/T_s\).

  • If \(f_s \geq 2W\): the copies don't overlap → we can extract the original with a lowpass filter ✅
  • If \(f_s < 2W\): the copies overlap (aliasing) → information is irreversibly lost ❌

Step 4 — Lowpass Filter Recovery

Apply an ideal lowpass filter with bandwidth \(W'\) and gain \(T_s\):

\[ H(f) = T_s \cdot \Pi\!\left(\frac{f}{2W'}\right) \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad X(f) = X_\delta(f) \cdot H(f) \]

Taking the inverse FT gives the reconstruction formula with sinc interpolation. ∎

Key takeaway from the proof

Sampling in time creates periodic repetitions of the spectrum. If these copies don't overlap, a lowpass filter can perfectly isolate the original. If they do overlap, you get aliasing — and no filter can undo it.

Aliasing & the Nyquist Rate

Aliasing — Why it's dangerous

When you sample below the Nyquist rate, high-frequency components "fold back" and masquerade as lower frequencies. This cannot be undone — the distortion is permanent. This is called aliasing distortion.

Prevention: Always use an anti-aliasing filter (a lowpass filter with cutoff at \(W\)) before the sampler.

Guard Band & Practical Sampling

In real systems, we sample at a rate higher than the Nyquist rate. The extra "breathing room" between adjacent spectral copies is called the guard band \(W_G\):

\[ f_s = 2W + W_G \]
Why do we need a guard band?

An ideal "brick-wall" lowpass filter is physically impossible. A guard band gives the reconstruction filter a gradual transition zone to roll off, making it realizable with real hardware. The wider the guard band, the easier the filter design — but the higher the sampling rate (and thus the data rate).

Worked Examples — Sampling

Example — Guard Band Calculation

Problem: A bandlimited signal has bandwidth \(W = 3400\) Hz. What sampling rate guarantees a guard band of 1200 Hz?

\[ f_s = 2W + W_G = 2(3400) + 1200 = \boxed{8000 \text{ Hz}} \]

This is exactly the sampling rate used for telephone-quality speech (8 kHz), where the audio bandwidth is 300–3400 Hz.

Example — Offset Sampling

Problem: If sampling starts at \(t_0\) instead of 0 (i.e., samples at \(t_0, t_0 + T_s, t_0 + 2T_s, \ldots\)), can we still reconstruct?

Solution: Yes! Define \(y(t) = x(t + t_0)\). Then \(y(t)\) has the same bandwidth, so sampling \(y\) at \(\{kT_s\}\) is equivalent to sampling \(x\) at \(\{t_0 + kT_s\}\). The reconstruction formula still works — the starting offset doesn't matter as long as the sampling rate satisfies the Nyquist condition.

Part II — Quantization

What is Quantization?

After sampling, each sample is a real number with potentially infinite decimal places. Quantization maps each sample to one of \(N\) finite levels.

\(x(nT_s)\)
Continuous amplitude
→
Quantizer \(Q(\cdot)\)
\(N\) levels
→
\(\hat{x}\)
Discrete amplitude
  • The real number line \(\mathbb{R}\) is partitioned into \(N\) disjoint quantization regions \(\mathcal{R}_1, \mathcal{R}_2, \ldots, \mathcal{R}_N\)
  • If sample \(x_i \in \mathcal{R}_k\), it is replaced by the quantization level (representative value) \(\hat{x}_k\)
  • Number of bits per sample: \(v = \log_2 N\)
Common confusion

"Region" vs "Level": A region is a range of input values (e.g., \([a_1, a_2)\)). A level is the single output value that represents the entire region (e.g., \(\hat{x}_2\)). There are \(N\) regions and \(N\) levels. The boundaries between regions are \(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{N-1}\) (that's \(N-1\) boundaries).

Scalar Quantization

In scalar quantization, each sample is quantized individually (as opposed to vector quantization, which processes blocks of samples together).

8-Level Quantizer Example

Consider 8 levels (\(N = 8\), so \(v = \log_2 8 = 3\) bits per sample):

Region \(\mathcal{R}_k\)RangeLevel \(\hat{x}_k\)
\(\mathcal{R}_1\)\((-\infty, a_1)\)\(\hat{x}_1\)
\(\mathcal{R}_2\)\([a_1, a_2)\)\(\hat{x}_2\)
\(\mathcal{R}_3\)\([a_2, a_3)\)\(\hat{x}_3\)
⋮⋮⋮
\(\mathcal{R}_8\)\([a_7, +\infty)\)\(\hat{x}_8\)

Notice: \(\mathcal{R}_1\) and \(\mathcal{R}_N\) extend to \(-\infty\) and \(+\infty\), respectively. These "outer" regions handle extreme input values.

Distortion & Signal-to-Quantization Noise Ratio

Quantization Error

The error introduced by quantization is:

\[ \tilde{x} = x - Q(x) = x - \hat{x} \]

Mean Squared Error (MSE) Distortion

Since the input \(X\) is a random variable, the average distortion is:

\[ D = E\!\left[(X - Q(X))^2\right] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - Q(x))^2 \, f_X(x)\,dx = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \int_{\mathcal{R}_i} (x - \hat{x}_i)^2 \, f_X(x)\,dx \]

SQNR (Signal-to-Quantization Noise Ratio)

\[ \text{SQNR} = \frac{E[X^2]}{E[(X - Q(X))^2]} = \frac{P_X}{D} \]
Think of it this way

SQNR tells you how much "useful signal" you have relative to the "rounding error." Higher SQNR → better quality. It's measured in dB: \(\text{SQNR}_{\text{dB}} = 10\log_{10}(\text{SQNR})\).

Uniform Quantizer

In a uniform quantizer, all interior regions have equal width \(\Delta\):

\[ a_{i+1} - a_i = \Delta \quad \text{for } 1 \leq i \leq N-2 \]

The quantization levels are placed at the midpoints of their regions, at distance \(\Delta/2\) from the boundaries.

Designing the Optimal Uniform Quantizer

The goal is to choose \(a_1\) and \(\Delta\) to minimize the MSE distortion \(D\). This is done numerically — there's no simple closed-form for most distributions. Tables of optimal values are provided for common distributions (e.g., zero-mean unit-variance Gaussian).

Students often ask

"How do I use the table?" — The tables give \(\Delta\) and \(D\) for a zero-mean, unit-variance Gaussian source. For a source with mean \(m\) and variance \(\sigma^2\):

  • Scale step size: \(\Delta_{\text{actual}} = \sigma \times \Delta_{\text{table}}\)
  • Scale levels: \(\hat{x}_{i,\text{actual}} = m + \sigma \times \hat{x}_{i,\text{table}}\)
  • Scale distortion: \(D_{\text{actual}} = \sigma^2 \times D_{\text{table}}\)

Nonuniform Quantizer (Lloyd-Max)

If we don't require equal-width regions, we have more freedom to minimize distortion. The result is a nonuniform quantizer that always performs ≥ as well as the uniform case.

Lloyd-Max Conditions

The optimal nonuniform quantizer must satisfy two conditions simultaneously:

Condition 1 — Nearest Neighbor Rule

Each boundary is the midpoint of its neighboring quantization levels:

\[ a_i = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{x}_i + \hat{x}_{i+1}) \]

This means: assign every input value to the nearest quantization level.

Condition 2 — Centroid Rule

Each quantization level is the centroid (conditional mean) of its region:

\[ \hat{x}_i = \frac{\displaystyle\int_{a_{i-1}}^{a_i} x\,f_X(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_{a_{i-1}}^{a_i} f_X(x)\,dx} \]

This means: place each level at the "center of mass" of the probability distribution within that region.

Iterative Design (Lloyd-Max Algorithm)

Start with an initial guess for the quantization regions \(\{\mathcal{R}_i\}\).
Compute the optimal levels (centroids) for the current regions using Condition 2.
Update the region boundaries using the new levels via Condition 1.
Repeat Steps 2–3 until the distortion \(D\) converges (stops changing significantly).
Why is nonuniform better?

A nonuniform quantizer puts more levels where the signal is most likely to be and fewer levels where the signal rarely goes. For a Gaussian signal (which hovers near zero most of the time), this means packing levels tightly near zero and spreading them out in the tails.

Worked Examples — Quantization

Example — Rate & Distortion (Gaussian Source)

Given: A stationary Gaussian source with \(\mu = 0\), PSD \(S_X(f) = 2\) for \(|f| < 100\) Hz (else 0). Sampled at Nyquist rate, 8-level quantizer.

Solution:

  • Bandwidth: \(W = 100\) Hz → Nyquist sampling rate: \(f_s = 200\) Hz
  • Variance: \(\sigma^2 = \int_{-100}^{100} 2\,df = 400\)
  • Bits per sample: \(v = \log_2 8 = 3\)
  • Bit rate: \(R = v \cdot f_s = 3 \times 200 = 600\) bps
  • Distortion: computed numerically ≈ 33.38 → SQNR = 400/33.38 = 11.98 ≈ 10.78 dB
Example — Uniform vs Nonuniform Comparison

For the same source (\(\sigma^2 = 400\), \(N = 8\)):

Part III — Encoding

Encoding — Bits to Levels

After quantization, each level \(\hat{x}_k\) must be assigned a unique binary codeword. With \(N = 2^v\) levels, we need \(v\) bits per sample.

\[ R = v \cdot f_s \quad \text{bits/sec} \]

Two Common Encoding Schemes

LevelNatural Binary (NBC)Gray Code
\(\hat{x}_1\) (lowest)000000
\(\hat{x}_2\)001001
\(\hat{x}_3\)010011
\(\hat{x}_4\)011010
\(\hat{x}_5\)100110
\(\hat{x}_6\)101111
\(\hat{x}_7\)110101
\(\hat{x}_8\) (highest)111100
Why Gray Code?

In Gray code, adjacent levels differ by exactly one bit. If a transmission error causes a single bit flip, the receiver decodes to a neighboring level — a small error. With natural binary, a single-bit error can cause a large jump (e.g., 011 → 111 changes level 4 to level 8). Gray code is more robust against channel errors.

Part IV — Waveform Encoding

PCM is the most fundamental waveform coding method. It's simply the full pipeline: Sample → Quantize → Encode.

Assumptions for Uniform PCM

  • Signal is bandlimited to \(W\) Hz
  • Amplitude is bounded: \(|x(t)| \leq x_{\max}\) for all \(t\)
  • Number of quantization levels \(N = 2^v\) is large
  • Uniform quantizer is used
Step Size for Uniform PCM

Since the input range is \([-x_{\max}, +x_{\max}]\) and there are \(N\) levels:

\[ \Delta = \frac{2x_{\max}}{N} = \frac{x_{\max}}{2^{v-1}} \]

PCM: SQNR & Bandwidth

Quantization Noise in Uniform PCM

When \(N\) is large, the quantization error is approximately uniformly distributed on \((-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]\):

\[ E[\tilde{X}^2] = \frac{\Delta^2}{12} = \frac{x_{\max}^2}{3N^2} = \frac{x_{\max}^2}{3 \cdot 4^v} \]

SQNR Formula — The "6 dB per bit" Rule

\[ \text{SQNR} = \frac{3 \cdot 4^v \cdot P_X}{x_{\max}^2} \]
\[ \text{SQNR}_{\text{dB}} \approx 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P_X}{x_{\max}^2}\right) + 6v + 4.8 \]
The famous 6 dB per bit rule

Every additional bit of quantization (\(v \to v+1\)) improves the SQNR by approximately 6 dB. This is because doubling the number of levels halves the step size, quartering the noise power.

Conversely, to improve SQNR by 25 dB, you need \(\lceil 25/6 \rceil = 5\) extra bits.

Common confusion

"What is \(P_X / x_{\max}^2\)?" — This ratio measures how "spread out" the signal is relative to its peak. For a uniformly distributed signal on \([-1, 1]\), \(P_X = 1/3\) and \(x_{\max} = 1\), so the ratio is 1/3. For signals that rarely hit their peak (like speech, which has a high peak-to-average ratio), this ratio is much less than 1, and the SQNR suffers.

Bandwidth Requirement

Each sample produces \(v\) bits, at a rate of \(f_s\) samples/sec:

\[ R = v \cdot f_s \text{ bps} \qquad \text{BW}_{\text{req}} = \frac{R}{2} = \frac{v \cdot f_s}{2} \]

At the Nyquist rate (\(f_s = 2W\)): \(\text{BW}_{\text{req}} = vW\). So PCM expands the bandwidth by a factor of \(v\).

Example — Full PCM Design (Example 10)

Given: Zero-mean WSS process, triangular PSD with bandwidth \(W = 5000\) Hz, \(x_{\max} = 600\), guard band = 2 kHz, \(N = 256\) levels.

  • \(P_X = \text{area under PSD} = \frac{1}{2}(10000)(1) = 5000\) W
  • \(f_s = 2(5000) + 2000 = 12{,}000\) Hz
  • \(v = \log_2 256 = 8\) bits
  • SQNR\(_{\text{dB}} = 10\log_{10}(5000/600^2) + 6(8) + 4.8 = \boxed{34.2 \text{ dB}}\)
  • Bit rate: \(R = 8 \times 12000 = 96{,}000\) bps
  • Bandwidth: \(\text{BW}_T = R/2 = 48\) kHz

To increase SQNR by 25 dB: Need 5 more bits → \(v = 13\), \(N = 8192\), new SQNR = 64.2 dB, new BW = \(13 \times 12000/2 = 78\) kHz.

Nonuniform PCM & Companding

Uniform PCM works well for uniformly distributed signals, but speech has a Gaussian-like distribution — small amplitudes are much more likely than large ones. Uniform quantization "wastes" levels on the rarely-used large-amplitude regions.

The compressor compresses the dynamic range (makes large values smaller), then a uniform quantizer is applied. At the receiver, the expander reverses the compression. The combined effect is equivalent to nonuniform quantization.

Two Standard Compander Laws

LawFormulaUsed In
μ-law \(\displaystyle g(x) = \frac{\ln(1 + \mu|x|)}{\ln(1 + \mu)} \cdot \text{sgn}(x)\) North America, Japan (\(\mu = 255\))

The idea in one sentence

Compress before quantizing → more effective levels near zero (where most speech energy lives) → better SQNR for speech signals without increasing the bit rate.

Differential PCM (DPCM)

Instead of quantizing each sample independently, DPCM quantizes the difference between consecutive samples.

Key Idea

Adjacent samples of most real signals are highly correlated — the difference \(Y_n = X_n - \hat{Y}'_{n-1}\) has a much smaller dynamic range than \(X_n\) itself. Therefore, fewer quantization levels (fewer bits) are needed.

\(X_n\)

The prediction \(\hat{Y}'_{n-1}\) is formed from previously quantized values (available at both transmitter and receiver), preventing accumulation of quantization noise.

Why not just use \(X_{n-1}\)?

Because the receiver only has access to the quantized version. If the transmitter subtracts the original \(X_{n-1}\) but the receiver adds back \(\hat{X}_{n-1}\), the mismatch causes errors to accumulate. DPCM avoids this by using the quantized prediction at both ends.

Delta Modulation (DM)

Delta Modulation is a simplified DPCM where the quantizer has only 2 levels: \(+\Delta\) and \(-\Delta\) (1 bit per sample).

How DM Works

At each sample, DM decides: "Is the signal going up or down?"

  • If the difference is positive → transmit \(+\Delta\) (one step up)
  • If the difference is negative → transmit \(-\Delta\) (one step down)

The reconstructed signal is a staircase that tries to follow the original.

The Step-Size Dilemma

The choice of \(\Delta\) involves a critical tradeoff:

Step SizeAdvantageProblem
Large \(\Delta\) Can follow rapid signal changes Granular noise — When signal changes slowly, output oscillates around the true value with big steps
Small \(\Delta\) Low granular noise for slow signals Slope overload — When signal changes rapidly, staircase can't keep up and falls behind
When does slope overload happen?

Slope overload occurs when the maximum slope of the DM staircase (\(\Delta/T_s = \Delta \cdot f_s\)) is less than the maximum slope of the input signal. To combat this, DM must sample at rates much higher than the Nyquist rate — this is the price of using only 1 bit per sample.

Major advantage of DM: Very simple hardware — only a 1-bit quantizer and a simple accumulator.

Adaptive Delta Modulation (ADM)

ADM solves the fixed step-size dilemma by adapting \(\Delta\) on the fly:

Adaptation Rule
\[ \Delta_n = \Delta_{n-1} \cdot K^{\epsilon_n \cdot \epsilon_{n-1}} \]

where \(\epsilon_n \in \{+1, -1\}\) is the quantizer output (before scaling), and \(K > 1\) is a constant.

  • Same sign (\(\epsilon_n \cdot \epsilon_{n-1} = +1\)): multiply by \(K\) → increase step size (signal is changing fast)
  • Opposite sign (\(\epsilon_n \cdot \epsilon_{n-1} = -1\)): multiply by \(1/K\) → decrease step size (signal is oscillating, probably near the true value)
Why this works intuitively

Two consecutive "up" steps (same sign) suggest the signal is rising fast and we need to take bigger steps to keep up. Alternating up-down steps (opposite signs) suggest we're oscillating around the true value — we should take smaller steps for finer tracking.

Worked Examples — PCM & Waveform Encoding

Example — Uniform Signal, 256-Level PCM

Given: Signal uniformly distributed on \([-1, 1]\), \(N = 256\).

  • \(P_X = \int_{-1}^{1} \frac{1}{2} x^2\,dx = \frac{1}{3}\)
  • \(x_{\max} = 1\), \(v = 8\)
  • SQNR \(= 3 \times 4^8 \times (1/3) = 4^8 = 65{,}536 \approx 48\) dB

Note: For a uniform distribution, the SQNR formula simplifies beautifully because \(3P_X/x_{\max}^2 = 1\).

Example — Achieving a Target SQNR

Given: Current SQNR = 34.2 dB with 8 bits. Need to increase by at least 25 dB.

Solution: Each extra bit gives ~6 dB. Need \(\lceil 25/6 \rceil = 5\) extra bits → \(v = 13\), \(N = 2^{13} = 8192\).

New SQNR = 34.2 + 30 = 64.2 dB.

Trade-off: bandwidth increases from 48 kHz to 78 kHz.
